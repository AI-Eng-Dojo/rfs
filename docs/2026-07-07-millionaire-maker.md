# Millionaire Maker

## Summary

Millionaire Maker is a web app that helps users design a rare career position by combining three specialties or professions. In this concept, "millionaire" means a one-in-a-million person, not necessarily a wealthy person.

The core idea is that instead of becoming the single best person in one domain, a user can aim for the top 1% in three different domains and combine them into a much rarer professional profile. The app asks assessment questions about strengths, weaknesses, traits, communication style, work preferences, and interests, then generates three high-fit specialties or professions.

The output is not just a list of jobs. It produces a tagline such as "a ___ who is good at ___, ___, and ___", explains the three-part combination, discusses why it could be valuable, and suggests first learning steps and small validation actions. If the user dislikes the result, they can regenerate another combination from the same answers.

## Problem

Career exploration often stops at the question, "What job suits me?" Many aptitude tests return a single conventional job title, such as sales, planner, engineer, or designer. They rarely help users combine multiple strengths into a differentiated professional position.

At the same time, modern work is harder to differentiate with one title alone. Value often appears at intersections: an engineer who can write, a data analyst who can sell, or a generative AI consultant who understands instructional design.

The user pain is:

- Aptitude tests return generic job names.
- Users do not know how to combine several strengths into a practical direction.
- People want career differentiation, but cannot decide which specialties to develop.
- "Become rare" is inspiring, but hard to translate into learning priorities and career hypotheses.

## Why Now

Generative AI makes it cheaper to produce personalized explanations, job combinations, taglines, and learning roadmaps from individual responses. Work itself is also becoming more fragmented and interdisciplinary, increasing the value of people who can connect multiple domains.

Career assessment and self-discovery content also travels well on social media. A product that answers "what three things should I combine to become rare?" can work both as self-understanding and as a practical career design tool.

## First Product

The MVP should be a web-based assessment and result generator.

- Assessment questions about strengths, weaknesses, interests, focus patterns, uncomfortable environments, communication style, abstract thinking, execution, and creativity
- Scoring that extracts the user's top three aptitude categories
- Specialty and profession candidates for each category
- A combined career hypothesis using three specialties or professions
- A tagline in the form "a ___ who is good at ___, ___, and ___"
- Explanation of the combination, its usefulness, possible markets, first learning themes, and 30-day experiments
- Regeneration of alternative combinations from the same assessment answers
- A simple share page or shareable image for the result

The first version should frame outputs as career hypotheses rather than definitive judgments. Until the assessment is validated, it should be a tool for self-understanding and exploration, not an automated decision-maker for hiring, school choice, or career transitions.

## Potential Users

Start with individuals who want to differentiate their career but do not know what to combine.

- University and vocational school students before job hunting
- People in their 20s and 30s considering a career change
- Individuals choosing a side-business or publishing theme
- Career coaches, schools, and training providers
- Small organizations that want to visualize team members' strengths

The first buyer could be an individual user, but B2B distribution through career schools, universities, corporate training, and coaching businesses may be more durable.

## Validation

The first signal is whether users feel the result is both personally accurate and worth trying.

- Percentage of users who save or share results
- Whether satisfaction improves after regeneration
- Percentage of users who start learning or publishing around one of the three generated areas
- Percentage who report after 30 days that the direction was useful
- Whether career coaches want to use it as a pre-session hypothesis tool
- Whether users see it as action-oriented career design, not just personality-test entertainment

## Open Questions

- Does the assessment measure real aptitude, or does it only produce flattering text?
- How should "top 1%" be measured when each domain has different populations and evaluation criteria?
- Will three-profession combinations help in actual hiring or client acquisition?
- The result users like may not be the specialty they should actually develop.
- Could "one-in-a-million" or "millionaire" sound like exaggerated advertising or a promise of success?
- How can the product avoid monetizing career anxiety?
- How should the profession and specialty taxonomy be updated while avoiding bias and stereotypes?
- What personal data should be stored, deleted, or reused after assessment?
